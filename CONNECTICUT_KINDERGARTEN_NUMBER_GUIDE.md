# Connecticut Kindergarten Number Guide

This guide is based on Connecticut's current Connecticut Core Standards for Mathematics, which the Connecticut State Board of Education adopted in 2010 and still publishes as the active standards framework.

## What matters most for kindergarten number learning

For kindergarten, Connecticut's standards put the strongest early emphasis on number. The key number-focused expectations come from:

- `K.CC` Counting and Cardinality
- `K.OA` Operations and Algebraic Thinking
- `K.NBT.1` Number and Operations in Base Ten

By the end of kindergarten, Luca should be working toward the following:

1. Count to 100 by ones and by tens.
2. Count on from a given number instead of always restarting at 1.
3. Recognize and work with written numerals from 0 to 20.
4. Count objects accurately and understand that the last number said tells "how many."
5. Count and compare groups, including deciding which group has more, less, or the same number.
6. Compare written numerals within 10.
7. Solve simple addition and subtraction situations within 10 using objects, drawings, fingers, sounds, or acting out.
8. Break apart numbers up to 10 in more than one way.
9. Find the partner that makes 10 for any number from 1 to 9.
10. Build fluency with addition and subtraction within 5.
11. Understand teen numbers as `10 ones and some more ones`.

## What the updated game now targets

- `Count It`: counting objects, counting on, and counting by tens.
- `Compare Numbers`: comparing written numerals and groups of objects.
- `Number Pairs`: number bonds, making 10, and simple add/subtract stories.
- `Teen Numbers`: composing and decomposing numbers from 11 to 19 as `10 + ones`.

## Important note

The app can support numeral recognition and number sense, but it does not replace off-screen practice with:

- writing numerals by hand from 0 to 20;
- counting real objects and touching each object once;
- talking through math thinking out loud with an adult.
